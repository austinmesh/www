  <h4>1. Why should I host an Austin Mesh radio node?</h4>
  <ul>
    <li>Your property becomes part of Austin's emergency communication backbone when storms or outages knock out cell service</li>
    <li>You join UT Austin, LASA, and AISD on a short list of partners making the city more resilient</li>
    <li>Costs you nothing, asks nothing of you, and quietly does good for your neighbors every day</li>
  </ul>

  <h4>2. What exactly is the node being used for?</h4>
  <ul>
    <li>Expands a free, open source, community mesh network using low-power radio</li>
    <li>Enables text-based communication in areas with limited cellular or internet coverage</li>
    <li>Used by AISD students to study weather patterns</li>
    <li>Often used for emergency readiness and local community support</li>
  </ul>

  <h4>3. What kind of equipment will be installed?</h4>
  <ul>
    <li>Typically a small, weatherproof box containing a microcontroller and battery</li>
    <li>A lightweight antenna and a solar panel</li>
    <li>Usually under 2 pounds and compact (roughly the size of your WiFi router, but the antenna is longer)</li>
  </ul>

  <h4>4. How will it be powered?</h4>
  <ul>
    <li>Nodes are solar-powered and self-sufficient</li>
  </ul>

  <h4>5. What kind of antenna or cabling will be involved?</h4>
  <ul>
    <li>Usually a small external antenna mounted to a mast or pole</li>
    <li>No cabling into the building.</li>
    <li>Typical installation uses either magnets, u-bolts around a mast or pole, or hose clamps. No drilling or screwing required.</li>
  </ul>

  <h4>6. What is the expected maintenance requirement?</h4>
  <ul>
    <li>Very low, nodes are designed to run autonomously for long periods.</li>
    <li>Occasional visits (once or twice a year) may be needed for firmware updates or hardware checks.</li>
    <li>Access terms can be set in writing when helpful, but we're generally flexible and plan weeks to months out.</li>
  </ul>

  <h4>7. What range and signal coverage will this node support?</h4>
  <ul>
    <li>Dependent on elevation, anywhere from 2–10 miles or more.</li>
    <li>Operates in the same frequencies as your garage door opener and baby monitor called the Industrial, Science, and Medical band (902-928mhz).</li>
  </ul>

  <h4>8. Will the equipment cause any interference with my existing systems?</h4>
  <ul>
    <li>All of the radios are FCC-compliant, meaning they go through testing for interference.</li>
    <li>It is _extremely unlikely_ as the network is both _ultra_ low power and operates on different frequencies from Wi-Fi, cell, or TV systems. Some water meters operate in the same frequency _range_, but are offset by >10mhz.</li>
    <li>Documentation and spec sheets are available for engineering review.</li>
  </ul>

  <h4>9. What are the liability implications?</h4>
  <ul>
    <li>The property owner carries the liability, though the likelihood of an incident is not material.</li>
  </ul>

  <h4>10. Are there any permits, HOA, zoning, or regulatory issues to be aware of?</h4>
  <ul>
    <li>Permits are not needed for small antennas and non-commercial installations.</li>
    <li>HOA or building rules may restrict roof modifications.</li>
    <li>We recommend written permission if you're a tenant or part of a shared property.</li>
  </ul>

  <h4>11. Will this increase the likelihood my property is struck by lightning?</h4>
  <ul>
    <li>No.</li>
  </ul>

  <h4>12. How long is the node expected to stay there? Can it be removed at any time?</h4>
  <ul>
    <li>We will work together to determine a clear timeframe or agreement as necessary.</li>
    <li>No long-term obligation required. You may request removal or remove it yourself at any time.</li>
  </ul>
